## Core Hypothesis

Gravity is not merely spacetime curvature, but represents a fundamental tendency toward **reduced orthogonal complexity** - a "pull toward the crystal U."

The gravitational force may be the macroscopic manifestation of a deeper principle: **the universe tends toward minimal orthogonal structure**.

### Refinement (2026-01-26)

**Gravity is emergent, not fundamental.**

The deeper principle: **Orthogonal dimensions "bump into each other" and reduce complexity.** This happens everywhere, universally. When multiple orthogonal things exist in proximity, they eventually interact and collapse toward fewer independent dimensions.

Gravity is simply what this universal orthogonality-reduction looks like when applied to **spatial/gravitational orthogonal dimensions** specifically. The same principle could apply to any pair of orthogonal dimensions - we call it "gravity" when it operates on the dimensions that give rise to spatial separation and mass-energy.

**Key insight**: Gravity isn't special. It's the emergent name for orthogonality destruction in the spatial domain. Black holes represent extreme cases where other orthogonality-destroying processes (nuclear forces, etc.) have already done their work, leaving only the spatial/gravitational orthogonality reduction to dominate.

### Further Refinement: Recrystallization

**Gravity IS recrystallization, not a pull toward it.**

The imperfect crystal (observable universe with perspectives/defects) is not static. It is actively trying to recrystallize - to heal its defects and return to perfect structure. This process happens **everywhere, all the time**, even deep inside the imperfect crystal.

Gravity is not "attraction toward a distant perfect state." Gravity is the **local act of recrystallization** occurring at every point in space. The universe is constantly trying to "heal" its orthogonal defects.

**Implications**:
- Every point in space experiences this recrystallization tendency
- Mass/energy represents "crystallization resistance" or "defect density"
- More mass = more defects = stronger local recrystallization pressure = stronger gravity
- Black holes = regions where recrystallization has succeeded locally (approached perfection)
- The universe isn't falling into black holes; rather, black holes are where the crystal has already healed

**New question**: What creates/maintains the defects (perspectives) against this constant recrystallization pressure? Why doesn't everything immediately collapse to perfect crystal?

### Possible Answer: Orthogonal Barriers Block Recrystallization

**[SPECULATION]** Recrystallization requires a "path" - you can't heal across too many orthogonal layers at once.

- High orthogonal complexity = many boundaries to cross = **stable against crystallization**
- The exterior can't crystallize inward because there's no path through all those orthogonal layers
- Each orthogonal boundary acts as a barrier

**Black holes as the exception**: When matter is compressed enough, orthogonal complexity is stripped away until only ONE orthogonal boundary remains. Now there IS a path - crystallization can proceed.

**Why we can't observe inside**: The black hole interior has shed all the orthogonal dimensions we share with it. We literally have no basis for observation - no common orthogonal axis to "see" along. The extreme Δt at the horizon represents this final severance of shared orthogonality.

**Picture**:
```
Normal matter:  [many orthogonal layers] → no recrystallization path → stable
Neutron star:   [few orthogonal layers]  → partial path → slow collapse
Black hole:     [one layer left]         → path exists → crystallization completes
Inside BH:      [no shared dimensions]   → unobservable to exterior
```

This would explain both the stability of ordinary matter AND the special status of black holes as "endpoints."
